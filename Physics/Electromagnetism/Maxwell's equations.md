**Maxwell's equations** capture classical electromagnetism by describing the relationship between [[electric field]] and [[magnetic field]]. They can be presented canonically as four [[partial differential equation]] in vector calculus terms. There are two homogeneous equations and two inhomogeneous equations.

|Name|Differential Form|Integral Form|
|---|-----------------|--------------|
|[[Gauss's Law]]|\begin{equation}\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho / \varepsilon_0\end{equation}|\begin{equation}\oint \mathbf{E}\cdot\dd{\mathbf{A}} = Q / \varepsilon_0\end{equation}|
|No Magnetic Monopoles|\begin{equation}\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0\end{equation}|\begin{equation}\oint \mathbf{B}\cdot\dd{\mathbf{A}} = 0\end{equation}|
|Ampere's Law|\begin{equation}\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{j} + \frac{1}{c^2}\pdv{\mathbf{E}}{t}\end{equation}|\begin{equation}\oint \mathbf{B} \cdot \dd{\mathbf{s}} = \mu_0 i\end{equation}|
|Faraday's Law|\begin{equation}\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\pdv{\mathbf{B}}{t}\end{equation}|\begin{equation}\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{E} \cdot \dd{\mathbf{s}} = - \int_S \pdv{\mathbf{B}}{t} \cdot \dd{\mathbf{A}} \end{equation}|
